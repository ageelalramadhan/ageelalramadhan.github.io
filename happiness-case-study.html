Global Happiness & Economic Factors (2019)

Python • Pandas • Matplotlib • Scipy • Tableau • Correlation analysis • Linear regression

Project Overview

This project explores what drives reported happiness across countries using the 2019 World Happiness Report. The analysis examines how economic and social indicators relate to happiness scores, with a focus on identifying which factors show the strongest and most consistent relationships at a global level.

Key Question

Is GDP the primary driver of national happiness — or do social factors play an equally important or stronger role? The answer has real implications for how governments prioritize policy.

Data

The 2019 World Happiness Report contains one observation per country (156 countries total), with happiness scores derived from the Gallup World Poll alongside six contributing factors: GDP per capita, social support, healthy life expectancy, freedom, generosity, and perceptions of corruption.

Methods

  • Exploratory data analysis (EDA) in Python — distributions, outliers, missing values
  • Scatter plots to assess relationships between happiness and each indicator
  • Pearson correlation analysis to quantify relationship strength
  • Linear regression for trend inspection (not prediction)
  • K-means clustering — tested but de-emphasized due to limited explanatory value
  • Tableau for interactive world choropleth map and scatter plot visualizations

Key Findings

  • GDP per capita shows a clear positive relationship with happiness — wealthier countries are generally happier
  • Social support showed a stronger and more consistent relationship with happiness than GDP alone
  • Countries with similar income levels differed significantly in happiness when social support varied — income doesn't fully explain wellbeing
  • Economic factors alone are insufficient to explain happiness; social conditions play a major role alongside income

Analytical Decision

The analysis initially compared GDP, social support, health, freedom, generosity, and corruption against happiness. GDP was examined first as a baseline — it shows a clear positive trend. However, social support emerged as the more consistent predictor: countries at similar income levels diverged significantly in happiness when social support differed. This shaped the final narrative focus on GDP and social support as the two most policy-relevant factors.

Limitations

  • Single-year snapshot — trends over time are not captured
  • Happiness scores are self-reported and may reflect cultural response biases
  • Correlations are observational — causation cannot be determined

Conclusion

Economic prosperity matters for happiness, but it is not sufficient. Social support — having people to count on — shows an equally strong or stronger relationship with reported wellbeing. This suggests that effective policy needs to address both economic and social dimensions, and that income growth alone is unlikely to maximize national happiness.
