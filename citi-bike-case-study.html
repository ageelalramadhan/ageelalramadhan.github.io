Project Overview

Citi Bike generates millions of trip records every year, but raw trip logs don't tell operations teams when to scale supply, which stations to prioritize, or how to plan for seasonal swings. This project takes 1.7 million individual ride records, merges them with NOAA daily weather data from LaGuardia Airport, and turns the combined dataset into an interactive, deployed dashboard built for operational decision-making.

Business question: How should Citi Bike scale bike supply and rebalancing operations across the year — and which stations, seasons, and environmental factors drive that decision?

1) Seasonality Drives Demand

Average daily rides follow a clear seasonal arc across 2022. Ridership climbs from a January low of around 33,000 rides/day, peaks at roughly 115,000 rides/day in late summer, then falls sharply as temperatures drop. The difference between peak and trough is a 3x+ multiplier in daily demand.

Key Insight — Seasonal scaling baseline: Winter demand (Nov–Apr) averages ~50% of summer demand (Jun–Aug). This directly quantifies how much redistribution intensity and staffing can be reduced in winter — and how early summer ramp-up should begin.

2) Temperature Predicts Daily Ridership

Overlaying daily ride counts with NOAA average temperature shows the two signals track each other closely across the full year. Cold snaps produce sharp drops; warm stretches push counts toward seasonal peaks.

Key Insight — Weather-aware operations: Temperature is a reliable forward-looking signal. Integrating NOAA daily forecasts into supply planning would allow operations to anticipate demand spikes before they arrive rather than reacting after the fact.

Code Highlight — Combining 12 Monthly CSVs Efficiently

A generator expression inside pd.concat() avoids building a list of 12 full DataFrames in memory. Each file is read, concatenated, and discarded before the next loads:

csv_files = [
    os.path.join(monthly_folder, f)
    for f in os.listdir(monthly_folder)
    if f.lower().endswith(".csv")
]

citibike_df = pd.concat(
    (pd.read_csv(file, low_memory=False) for file in csv_files),
    ignore_index=True
)

Code Highlight — NOAA API with Automatic Pagination

The NOAA CDO API caps responses at 1,000 records. The fetch function loops with an offset until fewer than 1,000 records return, guaranteeing the full year:

def fetch_noaa_daily(station_id, start_date, end_date, datatypes, token):
    all_results, offset = [], 1
    while True:
        r = requests.get(BASE_URL,
            headers={"token": token},
            params={"datasetid": "GHCND", "stationid": station_id,
                    "startdate": start_date, "enddate": end_date,
                    "units": "metric", "limit": 1000, "offset": offset,
                    "datatypeid": datatypes})
        results = r.json().get("results", [])
        all_results.extend(results)
        if len(results) < 1000:
            break
        offset += 1000
    return pd.DataFrame(all_results)

3) Demand Is Concentrated in Manhattan

All 10 highest-volume start stations in 2022 are in central Manhattan. The #1 station — W 21 St & 6 Ave — recorded 128,436 trip starts. The top 3 each surpassed 100,000 trips.

Key Insight — Priority rebalancing targets: These 10 stations are the highest-risk points for empty docks or unavailable bikes. They need more frequent rebalancing, higher dock capacity, and proactive early-morning restocking before commute peaks.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — Winter demand is ~50% of summer demand

Monthly averages show a 3x+ spread between peak (August, ~115K/day) and trough (January, ~33K/day). This directly quantifies the seasonal scaling opportunity for supply and staffing decisions.

Finding 2 — Temperature is a reliable demand predictor

Daily rides track average temperature closely across the full year. The scatter analysis confirms a strong positive trend. NOAA forecasts could be operationalized as a forward-looking demand signal.

Finding 3 — Top 10 stations are all in central Manhattan

The top station (W 21 St & 6 Ave) generated 128,436 trip starts in 2022. All top-10 are in a narrow Manhattan corridor — making them high-priority for dock capacity and rebalancing frequency increases.

Finding 4 — Spatial clusters confirm hotspots beyond station rankings

The Kepler.gl map reveals dense clusters in Manhattan's business district and the Hudson waterfront, with secondary clusters in Brooklyn — identifying movement corridors invisible in station-level aggregates.

Finding 5 — Generator-based loading is required at 1.7M row scale

Loading 12 CSVs naively would double memory usage. Using a generator inside pd.concat() streams each file sequentially — each is processed and released before the next opens. Essential for large-scale local data work without a database.
