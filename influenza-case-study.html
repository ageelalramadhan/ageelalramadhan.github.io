Influenza Mortality Analysis (2009–2017)

Excel • Tableau • Statistical analysis • Spatial visualization • Public health

Project Overview

This project analyzes U.S. influenza mortality data across all 50 states from 2009 to 2017, examining whether mortality rates changed over time and how population size affects observed death counts. The goal was to produce a statistically grounded, decision-ready view of influenza's impact for healthcare planning purposes.

Business Question

Did U.S. influenza mortality rates change significantly between 2009 and 2017? And how does population size influence the raw death counts observed across states? Understanding the difference between raw deaths and mortality rates is critical for fair cross-state comparison and resource allocation.

Data

The dataset covered U.S. influenza deaths and population data from 2009 to 2017, across all 50 states and five age groups: 0–4, 5–24, 25–44, 45–64, and 65+. Key metrics included total deaths and mortality rate per 100,000 population.

Process

  • Cleaned and validated mortality and population data in Excel
  • Calculated mortality rates per 100,000 to enable fair cross-state comparison
  • Compared mortality rate periods: 2009–2014 vs 2015–2017
  • Analyzed death distribution by age group
  • Built a two-sheet Tableau story — temporal trend chart and spatial U.S. map

Key Findings

  • No statistically significant change in overall influenza mortality rate between the two periods — it remained stable at approximately 0.012%
  • Strong positive correlation between state population size and total deaths (r ≈ 0.96) — larger states have more deaths simply due to exposure, not higher risk
  • Adults aged 75+ account for the largest share of influenza deaths across all states and years
  • Mortality rate is the correct metric for cross-state comparison — raw death counts are misleading

Why Mortality Rate Matters

Raw death counts are heavily influenced by population size. California and Texas have high death totals not because they are riskier, but because they have far more people. Switching to mortality rate per 100,000 removes this bias and reveals the true per-capita risk across states — a critical distinction for healthcare planning and resource allocation decisions.

Recommendations

  • Direct prevention resources toward adults aged 65+ as the highest-risk group
  • Use mortality rate rather than raw counts for cross-state staffing and supply decisions
  • Monitor state-level trends annually — stability at the national level can mask local spikes

Conclusion

This project demonstrates how statistical framing — choosing mortality rate over raw counts — fundamentally changes what the data says. A surface reading suggests some states are dramatically worse than others; the correct reading shows a broadly stable, age-concentrated pattern that calls for targeted, population-adjusted intervention.
